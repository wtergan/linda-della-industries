## Question
What can a new, technically strong Baltimore AI implementation studio sell honestly and deliver quickly enough to seek one $1,000 client in seven days?

## Primary-source findings

- NFIB’s 2025 technology survey reports that 24% of sampled small-business owners currently used AI, 42% were not at all familiar with operational or industry AI, and 63% expected AI to matter to their industry within five years. Among users, productivity was the most common reported benefit (30%); reported revenue and sales effects were much smaller. This argues for measured productivity/time-to-response positioning rather than guaranteed revenue claims. Source: https://www.nfib.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/2025-NFIB-Technology-Survey-1.pdf
- SBA Office of Advocacy analysis of Census BTOS data reports 8.8% of firms under 250 employees used AI to produce goods or services by the latest 2025 period. The narrower measure explains why it is lower than surveys that count tools such as Grammarly or Canva. Small firms lag most in robotic process automation, data analytics, chatbots, staff training, consultant integration, and data practices. Source: https://advocacy.sba.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Research-Spotlight-AI-in-Business-Small-Firms-Closing-In_-092425.pdf
- Maryland SBDC already provides no-cost AI advising, tool evaluation, and training. Linda Della should not lead with a generic paid audit or introductory class. The paid wedge must be implementation, customization, testing, and handoff. Source: https://www.marylandsbdc.org/consulting/technology-and-ai-advisory-services
- Maryland’s Online Data Privacy Act took effect October 1, 2025. It applies at defined processing thresholds and also governs processors working for covered businesses. Launch offers should minimize data collection, avoid sensitive/high-risk workflows by default, and state that legal/compliance advice is out of scope. Source: https://oag.maryland.gov/resources-info/Pages/data-privacy.aspx

## Competitive observations
Baltimore search results are crowded with broad “AI automation agency” pages and commoditized AI receptionist offers. Published competitor entry points range from roughly $197/month software to $7,500 one-time custom builds. Their pages often use unverified vendor claims and generic location SEO.

## Recommended wedge
**7-Day AI Workflow Sprint — $1,000 founding-client rate**

Deliverables:
1. One 60-minute workflow mapping call.
2. One production-ready, narrow automation in the client’s current tools.
3. Human approval gates for customer-facing or consequential output.
4. Basic privacy/data-flow notes and test scenarios.
5. Team handoff video or live walkthrough plus 14 days of fixes.

Good first workflows:
- web/email lead intake → structured qualification → response draft → owner approval → CRM/calendar
- shared inbox → classify/extract → task or spreadsheet → human review
- meeting/transcript → decisions/actions → follow-up draft → owner approval
- recurring document intake → extract fields → exception queue → human review

Avoid at launch:
- autonomous outbound calling
- healthcare/legal/financial decision-making
- claims of HIPAA/CMMC/MODPA compliance
- replacing employees
- open-ended “AI transformation” retainers

## Evidence quality
NFIB, SBA/Census, Maryland SBDC, and Maryland Attorney General sources are primary or government-backed. Competitor pricing and ROI statements are self-published and should be treated as positioning evidence, not proof of outcomes.
